Understanding loan types

The type of mortgage you choose has a big impact on how much you end up paying, both on a monthly basis and in the long term over the life of your loan. Knowing what type of loan is most appropriate for your situation prepares you for talking to lenders and getting the best deal.

A loan “type” is actually a combination of several different options:

  • Loan term
  • Interest rate structure
  • Loan program
= Loan type

The term of your loan is how long it will take to repay the loan.

There two reasons shorter terms can save you money:

  1. You are borrowing money and paying interest for a shorter amount of time.
  2. The interest rate is usually lower with a shorter term than with a longer term.

In general, shorter terms will save you money overall, but have higher monthly payments. There is no problem with getting a standard 30 year term, but if you can afford the higher monthly payment, you should consider getting a shorter term. Learn more in this short video.

Interest rate structure

Fixed rate or Adjustable rate mortgage

Interest rates come in two basic types: fixed and adjustable.

This choice affects: Whether or not your monthly payment amount can change, the amount of your monthly payment, and how much interest you will pay over the life of the loan.

Show

What are my choices for interest rate structure?

Fixed rate

  • Lower risk; No suprises
  • Higher interest rate
  • Rate and payments stay the same
Why might you choose this?
  • Your rate stays the same throughout the entire life of the loan.
  • You can calculate the exact amount of interest you will pay overall from day 1 of your loan.
Since 2008:

Chosen by 85-90% of buyers

Historically:

Chosen by 70-75% of buyers

Adjustable rate

  • Higher risk; Uncertainty
  • Lower interest rate to start
  • Rate and payments can increase over time
Why might you choose this?
  • Your initial rate may be less than a fixed rate mortgage.
  • After the fixed period, your rate will change not based on you, but on national data and a margin the lender adds.
Since 2008:

Chosen by 15-20% of buyers

Historically:

Chosen by 25-30% of buyers

Understanding ARMs

On your Good Faith Estimate (GFE) you will see two numbers:

5 / 1 Adjustable Rate

  1. How long your initial interest rate will stay fixed.

    Lenders can set this period to whatever they want, but the most common options are 3, 5, 7, or 10 years.

  2. How often your rate can change after the fixed period.

    Most lenders use this 1 year structure, but some offer other options. If your lender offers another option, make sure to read the terms and conditions.

In this case, a 5/1 adjustable rate means:
  • Your initial rate would be fixed for 5 years.
  • At each one of these yearly intervals you would recieve a new rate.
  • Your rate will change yearly starting in the 6th year of your mortgage.
  • Your new rate will be based on the market average, not your personal situation. Learn more

Loan program

Conventional, FHA, or Special programs

Loan options are organized into broad categories based on the size of the loan and whether or not they are part of a government program.

This choice affects: How much you will need for a down payment, the total cost of your loan (including interest and mortgage insurance), the house price range you can consider.

What should you expect in a loan program?

Many times the loan program is chosen for you based on the size of your loan, how much downpayment you plan to put down, and if you are a veteran. Ask lenders to give you multiple quotes so you can compare different options.

Conventional loan

Conventional loans generally have lower costs than FHA loans, but they require higher down payments and have stricter standards for approving loans.

  • Since 2008:

    Chosen by 65-70% of buyers

  • Historically

    Chosen by 85-90% of buyers

Conforming

Loan amount must be less than $417k. If your down payment is less than 20%, you will typically need to pay for private mortgage insurance.

Agency Jumbo

For loans more than $417k. Only available in certain counties, the maximum loan amount varies by county.

Nonconforming Jumbo

For loan amounts greater than the Agency Jumbo limit, this is the only option. Eligibility rules may vary from lender to lender. Good credit and high down payments are usually required.

While most conventional loans will fall into one of these three categories, lenders are not required to stick to these categories, so shop carefully.

FHA loan

The Federal Housing Administration (FHA) operates a mortgage program for borrowers with less cash for a downpayment or lower credit scores. The FHA doesn’t lend the money directly — private lenders do — but it provides insurance to the lender in case the borrower defaults on the mortgage.

FHA loans allow for down payments as low as 3.5%, but charge relatively high premiums for mortgage insurance.

  • Since 2008:

    Chosen by 65-70% of buyers

  • Historically

    Chosen by 85-90% of buyers

VA loan

VA loans are only available to veterans, surviving spouses, and active duty military members. If necessary, you can make zero down payment; it is also somewhat subsidized by the VA so will typically be cheaper than an FHA loan.

USDA loan

Also known as a Rural Development loan

USDA loans are only available in rural areas. Targeted to low/mod income folks. I think the selling point vs FHA/convention is just that it can sometimes be quite hard to qualify for an FHA/conventional loan in deeply rural areas, so this is an effort to expand access to credit in rural areas.

State and local programs

Targeted to low/mod income folks and first time homebuyers. Many of these programs would work in conjunction with an FHA/conventional loan. Offered by state housing finance agencies, local nonprofits, etc.

Understanding mortgage insurance

Mortgage insurance is typically required if your downpayment is less than 20% of the appraised value of your home. Mortgage insurance will increase the cost of your loan, so make sure to factor in this cost when comparing options.

Mortgage insurance — whether FHA or private — protects the lender in the event that you fall behind on your payments. It does not protect you. Your credit score will suffer and you may face foreclosure if you don’t pay your mortgage on time.
